CH 05/05
These terms describe how you know whether what you're doing is actually working — not to turn everything into a spreadsheet, but to make better decisions with less guesswork. A metric is only useful if it points to something you can act on.
T E R M S   01–04
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
What It Actually Costs to Bring Someone New In
// CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
Total cost — time, money, effort — to bring in one new client or customer. Divide what you spent by the number of new people who came in. Tells you whether your current approach is sustainable.
[+] EX (consulting)
If you spent forty hours on outreach and proposals over a month and brought in two clients, understanding the real cost of that acquisition helps you decide whether to invest in more passive channels instead.
What One Person Is Worth Over Time
// LTV — Lifetime Value
The total profit you can expect from one person across their full relationship with your work. Paired with acquisition cost, it tells you whether the economics make sense over time.
[+] EX (digital product)
If most buyers of your first product eventually buy a second — and some join a recurring membership — lifetime value is much higher than the initial purchase suggests. That changes how much it's worth spending to reach them.
How Often Interest Turns Into Something Real
// Win Rate / Close Rate
Of all conversations or enquiries that reach a certain stage, the percentage that become actual engagements. Tracks whether your qualifying conversations are improving over time.
[+] EX (design / build)
If you send ten proposals and three become projects, your close rate is 30%. Watching this over time shows whether you're consistently losing at a particular stage.
Whether Your Opening Is Doing Its Job
// CTR — Click-Through Rate
Clicks divided by people who saw something. Applied to a subject line, a headline, or a button — it tells you whether your opening is compelling enough to earn the next step.
[+] EX (digital product)
If only 1% of recipients click through to your product page, the issue is probably the email itself — the framing, or the relevance to where people actually are.
T E R M S   05–08
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Whether People Are Opening What You Send
// Open Rate
The share of people who open your email. Primarily a measure of whether your subject line and sender name are trusted and compelling enough to earn a look.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
If your open rate drops over time, it's worth asking whether you've drifted from writing things people find genuinely useful toward writing things that serve your agenda.
Whether People Are Actually Responding
// Reply Rate
The share who write back. In direct outreach this is the number that matters most — it represents actual human engagement, not just passive behaviour.
[+] EX (consulting)
If you send twenty outreach emails and one person replies, the question isn't whether you reached enough people — it's whether what you wrote gave someone a real reason to respond.
Which of Your Efforts Actually Led Here
// Attribution
The question of which touchpoint or channel deserves credit for a conversion. Always an approximation, never the full truth — but useful for knowing where to focus.
[+] EX (digital product)
Someone buys after a blog post, a newsletter mention, and a follow-up email. Knowing which of those three to invest in more is what attribution tries to answer — imperfectly, but usefully.
Replacing Guesswork With a Small Honest Experiment
// A/B Test
Running two versions of something against each other with enough volume to see which performs better. The point: stop assuming you know what works and find out instead.
[+] EX (design / build)
Testing two versions of the opening line on your contact page — one leading with process, one with outcomes — to see which generates more meaningful enquiries.
