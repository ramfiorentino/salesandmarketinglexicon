An adapted vocabulary guide
This lexicon takes the standard language of sales and marketing and reframes it for people whose primary motivation is their work — not the transaction. Every term has been renamed to describe its actual function, with the original industry term kept in brackets so you can use both languages fluently.
Who it's for
Consultants, coaches, designers, developers, and anyone selling a digital product who needs to understand the mechanics of connecting their work to the people who need it — without adopting the mindset of a salesperson.
How the terms are structured
Each entry has three parts:
ADAPTED NAME — what the term actually describes in plain language
// ORIGINAL TERM — the industry-standard name you'll encounter in the wild
DEFINITION — what it means and why it matters
[+] EXAMPLE — one concrete example from one of four service types
The four service types in examples
① CONSULTING — advisory or strategic engagements
② 1-ON-1 WORK — coaching, mentoring, therapy, tutoring
③ DESIGN / BUILD — client projects, commissioned work
④ DIGITAL PRODUCT — courses, tools, templates, software
Reading order
Chapters are designed to follow the natural arc of getting work to people: first understand who they are (Ch.01), then how to reach them (Ch.02), then what to say (Ch.03), then how to reach agreement (Ch.04), then how to measure what worked (Ch.05). You can also dip in anywhere.

// MAPPING THE PEOPLE AROUND YOUR WORK
VOL I OF I  ·  ADAPTED ED.
TERMS 09
STATUS LOADED
CH 01/05
Before you can connect your work with anyone, it helps to have a shared language for describing who those people are and where they stand. The terms below are a way of sorting — not of ranking people's worth, but of understanding how much common ground already exists between you and someone else. The closer the fit, the less explaining you have to do.
T E R M S   01 – 05
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Contact
// Lead
Anyone who has crossed paths with your work — a form fill, a reply, a handshake. The raw starting point of any list.
[+] EXAMPLE (digital product)
Someone who signed up for your newsletter after finding a free article through search. Interest shown; fit unknown.
Unknown Contact
// Cold Lead
Someone who hasn't encountered your work yet. You're introducing yourself from scratch — no prior signal, no shared context.
[+] EXAMPLE (consulting)
A company you've identified as a strong fit, whose operations lead has no idea you exist.
Already-Curious Contact
// Warm Lead
Someone who showed genuine interest on their own — visited, downloaded, was pointed your way. There's already a thread to pull.
[+] EXAMPLE (1-on-1 work)
Someone following your work for months who finally replied asking how your process works.
Relationship Contact
// Friendly Informal
Someone warm through relationship rather than marketing — a former collaborator, a friend of a friend. The warmth is personal, not earned through output.
[+] EXAMPLE (design / build)
A past client's co-founder who saw your work up close and now has their own product to build.
Good-Fit Contact
// Qualified Lead
Someone who, looking at what they need and what you do, lines up well. Right context, right problem, right circumstances. The filter that matters most.
[+] EXAMPLE (consulting)
A company with a specific operational challenge you've solved before and a decision-maker actively looking for help.
T E R M S   06 – 09
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Someone You're Exploring With
// Prospect
A good-fit contact now in real conversation. You're figuring out whether there's something real here. Not a commitment yet — but no longer just a name.
[+] EXAMPLE (1-on-1 work)
Someone you had an introductory call with who asked to speak again next week.
Your Ideal Person
// ICP — Ideal Collaborator Profile
A clear picture of the person who gets the most from what you do — their situation, their problem, how they think. Not about exclusion; about knowing who you're actually for.
[+] EXAMPLE (digital product)
"Professionals who are good at what they do but have never had to think about how to position or communicate it."
Where Someone Is With Your Work
// Lifecycle Stage
Where someone sits in their arc with you — just discovered, following along, worked with you once, or now referring others. Knowing the stage helps you respond appropriately.
[+] EXAMPLE (design / build)
A past client referring others is at a completely different stage than a first-time portfolio visitor — and needs a different conversation, if any.
A Group You Learn From Together
// Cohort
People who arrived at the same time or through the same path. Looking at them together reveals patterns — what they need, how they engage. Not for labeling; for learning.
[+] EXAMPLE (consulting)
Comparing year-one clients with year-three clients reveals how your ideal engagement has shifted and what kinds of work you do best.

C H A P T E R   T W O
// HOW PEOPLE FIND YOU (AND HOW YOU FIND THEM)
VOL I OF I  ·  ADAPTED ED.
TERMS 21
STATUS LOADED
CH 02/05
There's no single right way to get your work in front of the right people. The terms below describe the main shapes that process takes — some active, some passive, some that scale, some that don't. Knowing what each one is helps you decide where to put your energy.
T E R M S   01–07
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Reaching Out Directly
// Direct Outreach
You, writing to a specific person on purpose. One-to-one — you picked them.
[+] EX (consulting)
Emailing an operations lead, referencing a specific challenge they've publicly talked about.
Reaching Out Cold
// Cold Outreach / Cold Email / Cold Call
Direct contact with no prior relationship. Introducing yourself from scratch.
[+] EX (design / build)
Contacting a business whose product has an obvious gap — leading with a specific observation, not a generic offer.
An Introduction Through Someone You Both Know
// Warm Intro
Someone who knows you both makes the connection. Trust travels with them.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
A past participant mentions you to a friend going through something similar and connects you both.
A Recommendation
// Referral
Someone who knows your work sends a new person your way — passing their own credibility along.
[+] EX (consulting)
A client mentions you unprompted to another founder facing the same challenge.
People Finding You Naturally
// Organic Reach / Organic Marketing
Someone discovers your work without you pushing it — search, content, word-of-mouth. Slower to build; harder to kill.
[+] EX (digital product)
Your product gets mentioned in a newsletter you had nothing to do with because a reader found it useful.
Sharing Without Paid Promotion
// Organic Socials
Posting on platforms without paying to boost it. Reach comes from the work itself.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
A post about a stuck point you commonly see — framed so people feel recognised, not sold to.
A Gap Worth Filling
// Hole in the Market
A need unmet, a group poorly served, a question no one's answered clearly.
[+] EX (design / build)
A whole product category built by people who don't think about the person using it. You do.
T E R M S   08–14
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Scrappy, Creative Visibility
// Unconventional Outreach / Pirate Marketing
Low-budget, inventive ways of getting noticed when you lack resources for conventional promotion.
[+] EX (digital product)
Answering questions in a niche community so thoroughly that people start asking who you are.
Experimenting to Find What Reaches People
// Growth Testing / Growth Hacking
Small deliberate experiments to find out which approaches actually connect your work with the right people.
[+] EX (consulting)
Testing two ways of describing your offer to see which gets more genuine replies — not just opens.
Unexpected Presence
// Guerrilla Approach / Guerrilla Marketing
Unconventional tactics that catch people off-guard in a good way. Memorable because surprising.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
Leaving a long, genuinely useful answer on a public forum. No links. Just help. People notice who wrote it.
Collaborating to Reach Each Other's Communities
// Partnerships / Co-marketing
Adjacent work shared across both communities. You both bring something genuine; you both reach new people.
[+] EX (design / build)
A developer and designer agree to recommend each other when the situation calls for it.
A Series of Touchpoints Over Time
// Sequence / Drip Campaign
Planned messages sent gradually. The right thing to say depends on where someone is in their understanding.
[+] EX (digital product)
A short email series after someone downloads your free guide — each one going a layer deeper.
A Moment of Contact
// Touchpoint
Any instance where someone encounters your work. Meaningful connection rarely comes from a single moment.
[+] EX (consulting)
A potential client reads your article, sees you mentioned, attends your talk — by the time they reach out, it's begun.
Staying in Touch
// Follow-up
Reaching back out after an initial contact. Most conversations fade not from rejection — but from silence.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
Someone expressed interest then went quiet. A warm message two weeks later — no pressure — often restarts things.
T E R M S   15–21
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Staying Present for Someone Not Ready Yet
// Nurturing
Light, useful contact over time. You're there when they're ready. Patience, not pressure.
[+] EX (digital product)
A subscriber opening every email but not buying yet — continuing to send useful content is the right move.
The Path from Discovery to Engagement
// Funnel
Many people at the top, fewer at each stage, a handful who engage closely. Understanding the shape helps you focus.
[+] EX (design / build)
Hundreds visit your portfolio, fewer look closely, fewer reach out — a handful become clients. Each narrowing tells you something.
Early / Middle / Late in Someone's Journey
// TOFU / MOFU / BOFU
Where someone is with your work. What you share should match where they are — not where you want them to be.
[+] EX (design / build)
Overview for early discovery. Process breakdown for comparison. Testimonials for someone ready to decide.
Something Useful You Offer Freely
// Entry-Point Resource / Lead Magnet
A guide, tool, or resource shared openly. Genuinely useful on its own — not just bait.
[+] EX (digital product)
A free template solving a small but real version of the larger problem your product addresses.
A Focused Page for One Purpose
// Landing Page
One audience, one offer, one action. Everything on it serves that single purpose.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
A page just for people considering working with you — who it's for, what happens, what it costs, how to start.
What Someone Sees First
// Above the Fold
Visible before scrolling. If your core point isn't clear here, most people won't look further.
[+] EX (digital product)
Your product page must answer "what is this and who is it for" before anyone scrolls — or most will leave.
Getting Someone's Contact Information
// Contact Capture
The moment someone gives you a way to reach them. They're saying they want to stay in contact with your work.
[+] EX (design / build)
A potential client fills in the enquiry form. They haven't committed — but they've opened the door.

// WHAT YOU SAY AND WHY IT SHOULD BE YOU
VOL I OF I  ·  ADAPTED ED.
TERMS 16
STATUS LOADED
CH 03/05
A channel can bring people to the door. Only a message can bring them inside. These terms describe how to think about what you say — what you stand for, who you're for, and why you rather than anyone else.
T E R M S   01–08
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Where You Fit in Someone's Mind
// Positioning
The place you occupy relative to alternatives. Less about what you are; more about what you're next to — and what you're clearly not.
[+] EX (digital product)
A writing course for technical founders isn't "a writing course" — it's positioned against the experience of being able to build but not explain.
Your Core Reason to Choose You
// Value Proposition / Value Prop
A one or two-sentence answer to "why this, why you, why now?" Specific enough to resonate; clear enough to self-select.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
"I work with people changing direction professionally who need to think through what they actually want before making moves."
When the Work Starts Pulling Itself
// Product-Market Fit / PMF
The point where people are seeking your work out, coming back, and telling others — without you pushing. Before: you're figuring things out. After: you're keeping up.
[+] EX (consulting)
You stop chasing projects and start fielding inbound enquiries from people who heard about your work through others.
What Makes You Distinct — and What Keeps It That Way
// Differentiation / Moat
Differentiation wins the first client. The moat keeps them — it's what makes that distinction hard to copy quickly.
[+] EX (design / build)
Working only in one domain and understanding it deeply. The moat is ten years of domain knowledge a generalist can't replicate.
Your Sharpest Entry Point
// Wedge
A narrow first use case that's easy to say yes to — small enough to be low-risk, specific enough to demonstrate clear value.
[+] EX (consulting)
Offering a focused two-week audit of one specific process rather than pitching a full strategic engagement.
The First Group You're Actually For
// Beachhead Market
The specific, narrow segment you'll serve first and serve well — before expanding. The slice you can credibly own.
[+] EX (digital product)
Launching specifically for early-career UX designers who struggle to write case studies — not "anyone who wants to write better."
How Big the Space Is — and How Much Is Yours
// TAM / SAM / SOM
Three circles: everyone who could benefit (TAM), the portion you can reach (SAM), what you can realistically win near-term (SOM).
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
TAM: all professionals considering change. SAM: those in industries you know. SOM: the number you can actually serve well in a year.
Defining What You Do Rather Than Competing
// Category Creation
Naming a new kind of work rather than competing in an existing one. You set the standard others are measured against.
[+] EX (design / build)
Positioning as someone who does "operational design for small teams" — a frame you've defined, making direct comparison with generalists difficult.
T E R M S   09–16
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
The Opening That Earns Attention
// Hook
The first line or idea that stops someone mid-scroll. Its job isn't to explain — it's to earn one more second of attention.
[+] EX (digital product)
"Most people who are bad at pricing aren't bad at math. They're bad at believing their work is worth it." That's a hook.
A Brief, Clear Explanation of What You Do
// Pitch / Elevator Pitch
A short structured explanation of your work — what it is, who it's for, why it matters. Enough to spark genuine interest without slides.
[+] EX (consulting)
"I help ops-heavy businesses find where their process is costing them more than they realise — and fix it without adding headcount."
The Full Proposition
// Offer
Everything on the table: what they get, at what price, on what terms. Changing the offer often moves things more than changing the work.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
Adding a follow-up call two weeks after an engagement ends changes the offer meaningfully — without changing what you actually do.
The Three Jobs of a Page
// Headline, Subhead & Body
The headline earns a look, the subhead earns a read, the body earns the action. Each is doing something distinct.
[+] EX (digital product)
Headline: "Finally understand your numbers." Subhead: who it's for. Body: the detail for everyone still reading.
Evidence That Others Found It Valuable
// Social Proof
Testimonials, case studies, named clients. Borrowed trust, shown at the moment of doubt. Specific outcomes land; vague praise doesn't.
[+] EX (consulting)
A short case study describing a specific client's situation, what you worked on, and what changed — in their words where possible.
A Real Reason to Act Now Rather Than Later
// Urgency / Scarcity
Limited availability, a specific window, a cohort start date. Powerful when real; corrosive to trust when manufactured.
[+] EX (design / build)
You take a limited number of projects at a time and your next opening is in six weeks. That's real scarcity — say so plainly.
The One Thing You're Asking Someone to Do
// Call to Action / CTA
The specific next step you want a reader to take. Every piece of communication should have exactly one.
[+] EX (digital product)
A single button at the bottom of your product page that says "Start the course" — not three competing options.
Plainly Asking for What You Want
// Direct Ask
Stating clearly what you're hoping for — the meeting, the project, the decision. Underused because it feels blunt; effective because most people never do it.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
"I think this could be a good fit — would you like to start next month?" Rather than "let me know if you want to proceed."

// FROM CONVERSATION TO COMMITMENT
VOL I OF I  ·  ADAPTED ED.
TERMS 12
STATUS LOADED
CH 04/05
Reaching an agreement isn't usually a single moment — it's a sequence of smaller ones, each earning the right to the next. These terms name the stages of that process: not as tactics to deploy on someone, but as a shared language for understanding where a conversation is and what it might need.
T E R M S   01–06
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
The First Real Conversation
// Discovery Call
The initial conversation where you're not explaining yet — you're listening. Finding out if there's a real fit, what they need, what a good outcome looks like for them.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
A thirty-minute call before taking on a new client — asking where they are, what they've tried, what they most want to change. You're finding out if you can genuinely help.
Showing What You Do in Context
// Demo
A walkthrough of your work tailored to the specific person's situation — not a feature tour, but a demonstration of how what you do addresses what they're dealing with.
[+] EX (digital product)
Rather than a general product walkthrough, you show the specific module that directly addresses the problem they mentioned.
Responding to Hesitation Without Deflecting It
// Objection Handling
When someone raises a concern, the skill isn't having a rehearsed counter — it's taking it seriously enough to find what's actually behind it. The stated concern often isn't the real one.
[+] EX (consulting)
"The timing isn't great" might mean the budget isn't there, or there's internal politics around bringing in outside help. Asking which it is — genuinely — is more useful than reassuring them.
Testing Whether Someone Is Ready
// Trial Close
A low-stakes question that gives you a read on where someone actually stands — without asking for a full commitment. You're taking a temperature, not asking for a signature.
[+] EX (design / build)
"If we can agree on scope and timeline, is this something you'd want to start next month?" You're finding out if there's a real blocker.
The Moment of Decision
// Close
The point at which you ask for a decision and someone makes one. In a broader sense, the whole final phase — agreement, terms, confirmation it's happening.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
"I'd love to work on this with you — shall we confirm the start date and I'll send the agreement?" Simple, direct, names the actual next step.
An Invitation Rather Than a Push
// Soft Close
A gentle, low-pressure way of moving toward a decision — one that keeps the relationship intact if they're not quite ready.
[+] EX (digital product)
"If you want to get started, the link is below — and if you have questions first, just reply to this email." No urgency. No pressure.
T E R M S   07–12
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
A Direct Request for a Decision Now
// Hard Close
A more explicit push for a clear yes or no. Useful when a conversation has circled too long. Easy to misuse — risky with people who haven't had enough time to decide.
[+] EX (consulting)
"I need to know by end of week whether to hold this slot — I have another project that would take the same time." Direct and fair.
Moving Forward as Though the Decision Is Made
// Assumptive Close
Proceeding on the basis that the person is ready, without explicitly asking. Efficient when the signals are clearly there; presumptuous when they aren't.
[+] EX (design / build)
"I'll send the contract today — let me know if you want to adjust anything before signing." Moving the process forward while leaving room to correct course.
The Paperwork That Makes It Real
// Deal / Contract / LOI
A Letter of Intent is the "we plan to do this" document. The contract is the binding version. "Deal" is the whole agreement as a unit regardless of form.
[+] EX (consulting)
A statement of work outlining scope, timeline, deliverables, fees — signed by both sides. Not because of distrust, but because clarity protects the relationship.
Passing Responsibility to the Next Stage
// Handoff
The moment when one phase passes the relationship to another. Most friction happens at these transitions, when expectations haven't been passed clearly.
[+] EX (design / build)
The moment between a signed contract and project kickoff — when everything discussed in the proposal needs to transfer into the actual working relationship.
Any Time Someone Takes the Next Step
// Conversion
The moment a person moves from one stage to the next — signs up, books a call, makes a purchase. Always worth asking: converting from what, to what?
[+] EX (digital product)
Someone going from free newsletter subscriber to paying customer. Understanding what made it happen is more valuable than just counting that it did.
People Who Stop Engaging or Leave
// Churn
Those who end their relationship with your work. Some is inevitable. A lot of it is a signal about fit, expectations, or whether what you offered matched what people actually needed.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
Clients who expect quick answers and disengage before the work gets deep — that's not random churn. It points to a mismatch in how you describe your work.

These terms describe how you know whether what you're doing is actually working — not to turn everything into a spreadsheet, but to make better decisions with less guesswork. A metric is only useful if it points to something you can act on.
T E R M S   01–04
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
What It Actually Costs to Bring Someone New In
// CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
Total cost — time, money, effort — to bring in one new client or customer. Divide what you spent by the number of new people who came in. Tells you whether your current approach is sustainable.
[+] EX (consulting)
If you spent forty hours on outreach and proposals over a month and brought in two clients, understanding the real cost of that acquisition helps you decide whether to invest in more passive channels instead.
What One Person Is Worth Over Time
// LTV — Lifetime Value
The total profit you can expect from one person across their full relationship with your work. Paired with acquisition cost, it tells you whether the economics make sense over time.
[+] EX (digital product)
If most buyers of your first product eventually buy a second — and some join a recurring membership — lifetime value is much higher than the initial purchase suggests. That changes how much it's worth spending to reach them.
How Often Interest Turns Into Something Real
// Win Rate / Close Rate
Of all conversations or enquiries that reach a certain stage, the percentage that become actual engagements. Tracks whether your qualifying conversations are improving over time.
[+] EX (design / build)
If you send ten proposals and three become projects, your close rate is 30%. Watching this over time shows whether you're consistently losing at a particular stage.
Whether Your Opening Is Doing Its Job
// CTR — Click-Through Rate
Clicks divided by people who saw something. Applied to a subject line, a headline, or a button — it tells you whether your opening is compelling enough to earn the next step.
[+] EX (digital product)
If only 1% of recipients click through to your product page, the issue is probably the email itself — the framing, or the relevance to where people actually are.
T E R M S   05–08
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Whether People Are Opening What You Send
// Open Rate
The share of people who open your email. Primarily a measure of whether your subject line and sender name are trusted and compelling enough to earn a look.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
If your open rate drops over time, it's worth asking whether you've drifted from writing things people find genuinely useful toward writing things that serve your agenda.
Whether People Are Actually Responding
// Reply Rate
The share who write back. In direct outreach this is the number that matters most — it represents actual human engagement, not just passive behaviour.
[+] EX (consulting)
If you send twenty outreach emails and one person replies, the question isn't whether you reached enough people — it's whether what you wrote gave someone a real reason to respond.
Which of Your Efforts Actually Led Here
// Attribution
The question of which touchpoint or channel deserves credit for a conversion. Always an approximation, never the full truth — but useful for knowing where to focus.
[+] EX (digital product)
Someone buys after a blog post, a newsletter mention, and a follow-up email. Knowing which of those three to invest in more is what attribution tries to answer — imperfectly, but usefully.
Replacing Guesswork With a Small Honest Experiment
// A/B Test
Running two versions of something against each other with enough volume to see which performs better. The point: stop assuming you know what works and find out instead.
[+] EX (design / build)
Testing two versions of the opening line on your contact page — one leading with process, one with outcomes — to see which generates more meaningful enquiries.
