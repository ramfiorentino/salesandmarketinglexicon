// WHAT YOU SAY AND WHY IT SHOULD BE YOU
VOL I OF I  ·  ADAPTED ED.
TERMS 16
STATUS LOADED
CH 03/05
A channel can bring people to the door. Only a message can bring them inside. These terms describe how to think about what you say — what you stand for, who you're for, and why you rather than anyone else.
T E R M S   01–08
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
Where You Fit in Someone's Mind
// Positioning
The place you occupy relative to alternatives. Less about what you are; more about what you're next to — and what you're clearly not.
[+] EX (digital product)
A writing course for technical founders isn't "a writing course" — it's positioned against the experience of being able to build but not explain.
Your Core Reason to Choose You
// Value Proposition / Value Prop
A one or two-sentence answer to "why this, why you, why now?" Specific enough to resonate; clear enough to self-select.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
"I work with people changing direction professionally who need to think through what they actually want before making moves."
When the Work Starts Pulling Itself
// Product-Market Fit / PMF
The point where people are seeking your work out, coming back, and telling others — without you pushing. Before: you're figuring things out. After: you're keeping up.
[+] EX (consulting)
You stop chasing projects and start fielding inbound enquiries from people who heard about your work through others.
What Makes You Distinct — and What Keeps It That Way
// Differentiation / Moat
Differentiation wins the first client. The moat keeps them — it's what makes that distinction hard to copy quickly.
[+] EX (design / build)
Working only in one domain and understanding it deeply. The moat is ten years of domain knowledge a generalist can't replicate.
Your Sharpest Entry Point
// Wedge
A narrow first use case that's easy to say yes to — small enough to be low-risk, specific enough to demonstrate clear value.
[+] EX (consulting)
Offering a focused two-week audit of one specific process rather than pitching a full strategic engagement.
The First Group You're Actually For
// Beachhead Market
The specific, narrow segment you'll serve first and serve well — before expanding. The slice you can credibly own.
[+] EX (digital product)
Launching specifically for early-career UX designers who struggle to write case studies — not "anyone who wants to write better."
How Big the Space Is — and How Much Is Yours
// TAM / SAM / SOM
Three circles: everyone who could benefit (TAM), the portion you can reach (SAM), what you can realistically win near-term (SOM).
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
TAM: all professionals considering change. SAM: those in industries you know. SOM: the number you can actually serve well in a year.
Defining What You Do Rather Than Competing
// Category Creation
Naming a new kind of work rather than competing in an existing one. You set the standard others are measured against.
[+] EX (design / build)
Positioning as someone who does "operational design for small teams" — a frame you've defined, making direct comparison with generalists difficult.
T E R M S   09–16
* * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * * *
The Opening That Earns Attention
// Hook
The first line or idea that stops someone mid-scroll. Its job isn't to explain — it's to earn one more second of attention.
[+] EX (digital product)
"Most people who are bad at pricing aren't bad at math. They're bad at believing their work is worth it." That's a hook.
A Brief, Clear Explanation of What You Do
// Pitch / Elevator Pitch
A short structured explanation of your work — what it is, who it's for, why it matters. Enough to spark genuine interest without slides.
[+] EX (consulting)
"I help ops-heavy businesses find where their process is costing them more than they realise — and fix it without adding headcount."
The Full Proposition
// Offer
Everything on the table: what they get, at what price, on what terms. Changing the offer often moves things more than changing the work.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
Adding a follow-up call two weeks after an engagement ends changes the offer meaningfully — without changing what you actually do.
The Three Jobs of a Page
// Headline, Subhead & Body
The headline earns a look, the subhead earns a read, the body earns the action. Each is doing something distinct.
[+] EX (digital product)
Headline: "Finally understand your numbers." Subhead: who it's for. Body: the detail for everyone still reading.
Evidence That Others Found It Valuable
// Social Proof
Testimonials, case studies, named clients. Borrowed trust, shown at the moment of doubt. Specific outcomes land; vague praise doesn't.
[+] EX (consulting)
A short case study describing a specific client's situation, what you worked on, and what changed — in their words where possible.
A Real Reason to Act Now Rather Than Later
// Urgency / Scarcity
Limited availability, a specific window, a cohort start date. Powerful when real; corrosive to trust when manufactured.
[+] EX (design / build)
You take a limited number of projects at a time and your next opening is in six weeks. That's real scarcity — say so plainly.
The One Thing You're Asking Someone to Do
// Call to Action / CTA
The specific next step you want a reader to take. Every piece of communication should have exactly one.
[+] EX (digital product)
A single button at the bottom of your product page that says "Start the course" — not three competing options.
Plainly Asking for What You Want
// Direct Ask
Stating clearly what you're hoping for — the meeting, the project, the decision. Underused because it feels blunt; effective because most people never do it.
[+] EX (1-on-1 work)
"I think this could be a good fit — would you like to start next month?" Rather than "let me know if you want to proceed."
